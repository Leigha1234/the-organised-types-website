Rock Bottom Wasn’t the End — It Was My Launchpad

There were nights I sat in my car at 2 a.m., gripping the steering wheel, heart racing, unable to breathe under the weight of it all. Not just the business failing — the heartache, betrayal, and constant pressure, the feeling of letting my family and myself down, that made me question everything. Partners I trusted breaking promises. Clients disappearing when I needed them most. Tight cashflow and wages I couldn’t cover. Extortion situations disguised as negotiation.

Eventually, I broke. I had a full breakdown! Crying alone in my office, my car, even the bathroom, my husband and children pulling me out of severe panic attacks. I doubted myself, my abilities, and my right to keep going.

I'd hit rock bottom, mental breakdown and perimenopause all at the same time. A truly horrible place that I never imagined I’d reach.

The "Do Something" Principle

And yet, from that low point, I learned something critical. I had only two choices: give up, or do something. Waiting for perfect timing or perfect circumstances wouldn’t save me. That’s when I discovered a concept that hit me like a lightning bolt in The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck by Mark Manson (@markmanson): the “Do Something Principle.”

Manson’s book taught me that life isn’t about avoiding pain or failure, it’s about choosing what matters enough to care about, and taking action even when it’s hard. Once I embraced that, I started moving.

It wasn't easy but here’s what that looked like for me:

  • I made the calls I dreaded. Banks, clients, partners — each conversation lifted a weight from my chest.
  • I talked, shared, and created, even when I doubted anyone would notice. Every blog post, email, or small marketing experiment built momentum.
  • I owned my mistakes, accepted my reality and pivoted. Failures became lessons instead of shameful endings.

It wasn’t instant. The heartbreak lingered. The betrayals still stung. But slowly, momentum grew, and small wins — a client saying yes, a campaign working, or just surviving another day — reminded me that action breeds confidence.

Here’s what I want other women business owners to take from my journey:

Do something, even when you feel broken. One small step today beats waiting for perfection tomorrow.

Choose what to care about. Not everything deserves your energy or stress. Focus on what aligns with your values.

Failures are feedback, not punishment. Every setback is an opportunity to learn and pivot.

You’re not alone. I’ve been at the edge — so have countless women entrepreneurs. You can survive and thrive too.

And here’s the part I want you to know: you don’t have to face it alone. So, take action. Write that post. Make that call. Send that email. It might feel terrifying, but every small step proves you’re still moving forward.

Rock bottom isn’t the end. It’s the launchpad. And sometimes, the darkest nights show you the way to the brightest dawns.
